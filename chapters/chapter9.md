<h3>750, 7th of Quadedre</h3>

“Whew, finally made it out of the woods\! Ahh, what fresh sunshine, eh, Rama?” Ardhari stretched his arms open to greet the afternoon sun.   

Ardhari and Ramanari’s two week stay in Xaede had come to an end yesterday; the last of their wares had flown off. The usually heavy satchels and bags on their backs dealt no burdens to slow down their next move: to restock on supplies at Crustadolh, the harbor town located northwest from there and closer to the Northern Kingdom's territory.  

Though Ardhari was happily basking in the sun as they walked, Ramanari could only look back at the forests behind him with foreboding apprehension.  

“Will Miss Loka make it in time to meet us?” he asked Ardhari.  

“Hm? Not like you to doubt her.”  

“It is not her I doubt, but the circumstances.”   

“If this is about the voyage, you still don’t get what she can do. She likes the sea’s waters as she likes her men.”  

“What, men made of razor icebergs and lightning?”  

“No, I mean she likes them rough and stretching for leagues, obviously.” Ardhari smugly corrected.  

“Oh, shut up.” Ramanari rolled his eyes. “That is not the concern; remember the whispers of Fleetway Verde’s presence?”  

“Yes, the whispers also told of the bloody skirmishes between those pirates and Xaede’s army. Dangerous at the time, but now? They can do naught but lick their own wounds. Thus, we hurry on this road and make it to Crustadolh while they do.”  

“Ardh, you and I heard about one of the Xaedean princesses and her squadron on the brink of death. We’ve seen their weapons and how their royalty fights. If even they struggled against those rogues, then…” Unwilling to commit to cynicism, Ramanari dared not finish the sentence.  

“Rama. We’ve heard they’re in too many places at once; these brigands are spread thin. And it’s not like they can just assemble members back at the seas or find new recruits in a flash. True pirates carry too much vim to dark join under the banner of one whose name in creative terms stands equal to rat shit.”  

“You’re…especially confident in Miss Loka and I.” Ramanari was unsure how to feel about the extra bravado Ardhari sharpened himself with.   

“I mean, yeah\! If not for that, ARKH would be 6 leagues in a ditch, and I’d die a beggar\!” Ardhari shouted.  

Ramanari picked up a low growl far behind them and immediately gripped his scimitar machete. Its presence emanated a dark gleam at the tightening of his grip, as if it were an extension of his arm.  

“...I won’t let you down, then.”  

A panther of pitch black vice quietly unsheathed its invisibility; it had stalked them for several leagues through the forest. Ramanari had made sure to tire its patience while luring it out of the glades and into the shining grass and plains. Right when it leapt forward–  

**“ROOOAAAHHH–\!\!\!”**  

**SHING. SHWOOM.**  

Silently, he shredded through its flesh in three swings around its body, then–   

**SHPLITCH.**  

He stabbed it into the ground with a fourth\!   

The beast, now fearful that it became the hunted, let out a cry of agony\!  

The hunter spared no time decapitating its head.  

The merchant, his brother, whistled out of respect and surprise.  

“See, Rama? You’ll do fine. That *obsidanther* never stood a chance.” he encouraged.  

They collected his head and other remains to be used for future materials.   

The hunter, however, could not cleanse the apprehension from his mind.  

“Don’t hold your breath, brother. Ever since we’ve come to this side of Axiom, I’ve found matches galore for this blade.”  

Ardhari, now serious, looked back at the glades they had once traveled through, and nodded. He too, began to contemplate.  

“Shenael Lunendra, eh? I’d hate to be on the end of her fists. If that strength’s commonplace around the North, then…”  

The two were immediately back to walking; Ardhari’s voice rose higher as a declaration to the heavenly sky above.  

“...It’ll be decades until ARKH’s name gets carved into Axiom’s gold.”

*Ho there, dear audience. My name is Pendra Ashwright, seventh successor to my name. And currently…*  

“Right this way, Lord Pendra.” said one of the guards for the royal dungeon.  

*One week has passed since my spring vacation from Katorus Academy started\! I was looking forward to a fun family reunion back in my chilly home; Father, Mother, and all 8 of us were together for some good old family quality time, what could go wrong? Well…*  

Back in the Northern Kingdom, in the underground where prisoners were often held, the seventh successor was but a visitor. Though he came in commoner’s clothes and a cloak, the guards knew better than to disbar young royalty such as him. Two of them took up the tasks to escort him, but perhaps fear towards the one he planned to visit was why their pace became slow and careful. The only way Pendra could cope with such palpable tension was to look back on everything that led to this point.  

*…Let’s just say a whole slew of things went wrong days ago, and now I’m spending a nice day observing the dungeons. Sheesh, brother, did you have to give these two poor saps a hard time, too?*  

“Here’s the soon-to-be exile you requested to see, Lord Pendra.” said the other guard.  

Pendra followed her gesture and looked upon the cell. Bare necessities of a chamber pot, a bucket of washing water, and bed aside, the cell was a stone cold abode occupied only by Mercer wearing nothing but loose pants and doing pushups. Pendra was too distracted by the magic burns across his entire body to notice the puddle of sweat below the irritable prisoner. The burns ran like raging rivers, marring his shoulders, thorax, and almost his entire back.   

*Been a while since I’ve seen those; they look fresh and old at the same time. Is he constantly reopening them with his flames? I don’t recall that being necessary to awaken his Root…unless…he’s driving himself with the pain?*   

“Procedures dictate that this consultation must be no more than 15 minutes, Lord Pendra. Be quick in what you must say to this one.” said the other guard.  

*Fifteen minutes won’t be enough to talk him out of the self-immolation spell.*   

“That’s fine. It won't be long, you two can stand back for a bit.” he replied.  

The two guards nodded and moved out of Mercer’s sight on opposite ends, still keeping an eye on Pendra.   

*Keep it simple.*  

“Ho there, Mercer. They feeding you well?”  

Mercer did not respond to him, for his intense focus was on the floor.  

“Figured we’d have one last chat before you’re sent off tomorrow.”  

Still, he did not answer.  

“Don’t you have a thing to say here? You may regret it if you don’t.”  

Mercer briefly stopped his pushups and a low growl bubbled from his throat. “No.”  

“You sure?”  

Mercer continued to thoroughly push into the floor and back out of it, his voice only delivering grunts with each rep.  

“Alright, fine, have it your way.” Pendra sighed. “I just have a question for you about that stunt you pulled on Gerwyn. Was it all a plan for this?”  

The pushups finally stopped, but the prisoner’s focus did not divert. He wiped some sweat off with a towel and put his plain shirt back on.  

“Were you trying to instigate Father with that fight? And all those insults you threw around? I know what you are, but–”  

“Oh, *do you*?” Mercer finally responded. He threw the towel to the bed and sauntered closer to Pendra, fastening two iron grips on the bars.

“Then answer this, *Penny*. Why do you *think* I resort to these lengths?” he asked, expression glowing in its pure lividity.  

“Uh…because you keep waking up on the wrong side of your bed?”  

“You’re one of the unfunniest people to ever walk this soil.”  

“Alright, alright, the answer’s because…you needed some way to reach the Highlands without getting disbarred by the gate there. Though if you wanted to do that, you could have asked Father for permission.”  

“He’d never allow that so flippantly. Least of all to me.”  

“Maybe if you took those chances to prove yourself a better man without letting that *foul temper* squander your chances, then we'd be telling a different story outside of these dungeons.” Pendra stated while casually gripping one of the cell bars.  

Mercer mocked Pendra with a querulous, child-like tone. “‘*Maybe if you took those chances to prove yourself a better man*–’ with what, *damn it*\!?” He slammed a foot onto one of the bars. “You want me to pull the weeds out of his garden and lick the filth off everyone’s boots for a sliver of some good graces\!? Give me a break with the fairy tales\!”  

“No, that’s not what I mean at all, and no, this doesn’t call for a break; you’re sending yourself to an early grave\!” Pendra reached the verge of exasperation. “I mean…the Highlands, brother\! Why pine for the Highlands? I’ve been there before on that trial of passage, and the whole test you gotta do to survive for two weeks is dangerous enough. You’re gonna be there for *seven* months\!”  

“Yeah, so what?” Mercer let go of the bars, grabbed his towel, and moistened it with water.  

The lack of hesitation in his question startled Pendra.   

*What do you mean “so what?”*  

The thoughts latched on to his voice and flowed out through instinct. “Do you…want to die that badly–”  

“No, *dumbass*. I need to get stronger, and being here with the rest of you *Ashwrights* isn’t going to help that at all.”   

“We’re constantly sparring with each other and being asked to defend ourselves in sticky scenarios. What about those?”  

“What *about* those?”  

“Geh?”  

Mercer groaned at Pendra’s cluelessness whilst rubbing his face with the towel. After he freshened up, he dried it with magic heat from his hands and tossed it to the bed. With a cleaner face that better exposed his cross, glaring eyes, he looked straight into his younger brother on the opposite side of the cell.  

“Pendra. If someone could become a swordsman by swinging a sword at the air the right way for months on end, would this world really need knights? Would anyone bother pursuing strength if it was *that* simple?   
The whole reason we’re all taught how to fight isn’t to fulfill some theatrical performance for no-name nobles to gawk at, it’s so we can learn how to kill. You don’t improve at killing with spars, you improve at killing with *killing*. Why do you think we’re all sent to the Highlands in our 12th year to fight the wilderness? You give it three seconds of thought and the answer shows itself; because that time, where it’s kill or be killed, does a lot more for our experience than playing around with sticks and scores will.”  

*Seven be damned to hell and back, not every soldier’s a bloodthirsty masochist.*  

Pendra did not look away, not from the diatribe or the terror in the cage. “We do practical regimens with Ashwright’s soldiers as much as the spars, though. Did you conveniently forget about all those marches and mock war battles we have to take part in?”   

Mercer gripped a bar with one hand. “Those aren’t enough, and they don’t happen enough. So what if this forsaken kingdom can re-enact full battles with its own army? No matter how hard you swing a wooden sword, a war game will *never* be more than a game.”  

Pendra let go of the bar, but held firm. “Okay, sure. But no matter how confident you are against the Highlands’ wilderness, suicide will never be more than suicide.”  

“Not if you still remember the layout and where the beasts’ dens usually settle.” Mercer tapped his right temple with a finger.  

“The Highlanders? It’s not uncommon for them to kill trespassers on sight. Especially if they’re Ashfolk.”  

“Not if you know how to kill them on sight *first*.” Mercer tapped his right temple again.  

“*Blizzards*. Your hide is *not* surviving seven months in the center of those. Oh, and don’t say some hogwash like ‘not if you know how to turn yourself into a bonfire in the middle of it, though.’” Pendra mocked Mercer’s contemptuous tone while tapping his own right temple with a finger.  

“Hey, how about you give it up already? I’m behind bars, idiot; all you’re doing is pushing a wet boulder up a mountain.” The statement did not shake Mercer; the most he gave was a scowl obfuscated by the cell’s darkness.   

“Well, nothing *you say* here’s going to stop me from sending a farewell.” Pendra already knew the futility of his actions deep down, but his cognition always viewed futility as a bane.   

“Go find something better to do with your life, Pendra.” Mercer lay atop his bed in prison. “You’re not gonna carve a legend sticking around in this forsaken dump.”  

Pendra shrugged. “Personally, I’d rather the first legend I carve be something cool and exciting. Y’know, the type that doesn’t involve picking unnecessary fights to guarantee an early grave.”  

“Do it while I’m gone.” Mercer turned over to his left side, preferring to sleep with the wall in sight instead of his younger brother.  

The unease sunk deeper into the air they inhaled.  

“One minute remains, Lord Pendra,” said one of the guards.  

*…Yeah, no.*  

“Well, that’s that, then. Farewell, brother.” Pendra waved before letting the guards escort him back out of the prison. Unsurprisingly, Mercer did not wave back.  

Once Pendra had said his goodbyes to the prison guards, he stretched his arms and yawned before the coming night.   

*Carving a legend, eh…*  

The young, casual 7th successor had gone past the point of anguish and began to move on.  

*Gerwyn’s punch definitely lit a fire in him. This wet boulder wasn’t worth the shove.* 

It had been a while since Pendra found time to contemplate the goal of his dreams.  

*Speaking of which, I haven’t seen him since then. How he made Mercer bleed is one thing, but…*  

He looked upon the looming stairs and the castle’s structure that embraced the coming darkness on his way inside.  

*What’s he doing now?*

Gerwyn was in the Ashen Archives, as was oft.  

Archive Master Cen Francis, the one he was looking for, was fortunately around the front desk and instructing the receptionist of something he dared not eavesdrop on. She noticed him right afterwards, fixed her golden spectacles and dark hair, and wasted no further time approaching him.  

“Good evening, Master Cen.” he greeted.  

“Good evening to you too, Lord Gerwyn. You have arrived just in time.” She bowed, then flipped through her notepad again. “We’ve just managed to fulfill your request yesterday to find the origins behind that strange book *Knuckle Duster*. However…”  

“...Was there little to be found?” asked Gerwyn.  

“Regrettably, yes. The text was the only one authored by this ‘Bobby West’ person, so there was nothing to trace back to. Do you have the book with you now?”  

“Yes, but…” Gerwyn lowered his voice to an audible whisper and came closer to Cen. “The truth is…the foreign make of it is worrying to keep in the light.”  

It did not take above a second for her to catch on. She turned to the young receptionist at the front desk and shook her head, incurring her to nod and plug her fingers into her ears.   

He carefully took *Knuckle Duster* out of his satchel and handed it to her. She flipped through its pages and carefully felt out the texture of the pages, frowning as the dots came together.   

*If there’s anyone who can see the implications behind this, it’s Master Cen.*  

“...You’re right. I’ve never seen anything like this before.” Cen handed the book back and whispered. “Keep it close at all times.”  

“Do you have the name of the one who turned this into the Archives?” Gerwyn kept his voice low while putting the book away.  

“He submitted it to us recently, so finding that name took no time at all. The one who brought *Knuckle Duster* here…was Lord Georg Ashwright.”  

**THUMP.**  

The name forced him to step back on instinct.  

“G-Georg?”  

Cen flipped another two pages in her notepad. “Yes, it was one of several articles lent to us that day. The receptionist who he met reports that he ‘felt like giving away some souvenirs after so long.’”  

*No ‘souvenirs’ get brought into these archives, there’s an approval process to go through. Unless…did he bribe his way into skipping it?*  

“Though…it was under confidentiality; only Master Zetz knows the origins of the books. My sincerest apologies, Lord Gerwyn, but this is the most I can tell you.” Cen was familiar enough to figuratively read his mind.  

*Then Georg…he knows the implications of this book better than I do. But Archive Master Zetz Niall? I know little of him too, but were those two close this whole time*   

“No worries, I...understand.” answered Gerwyn. “But what of the other books he sent?”  

“Most are in Master Zetz’s possession, whilst others were relegated to Floor 0.”  

**THUMP.**  

The implications behind why such texts would be locked behind that imposing door of cold obsidian were of the sort Gerwyn strained to remove from his mind, but to little avail.  

“Now, with all that said and done, is there anything else you need for the day?”  

“Um…no, there isn’t. That’s all I came here for.”  

The conversation suddenly stood at a silent impasse.   

*Georg has already done much to ensure the secrecy of these texts.*  

Cen signaled the receptionist to unplug her ears, though a part of her wished she didn’t.  

*So now, keeping Knuckle Duster out of sight is an absolute priority. Who knows what I could get roped into if he finds out I have it?*  

That side of her came not from anything done by her subordinate, but the contemplating prince in front of her.  

*But is that possible? Georg’s social connections stretch deep into this kingdom and far across the rest of Axiom; anyone here could ascertain that. How many eyes do I have to–*  

“Does a concern ail you, Lord Gerwyn?”  

**THUMP.**  

Gerwyn snapped out of his trance. The uncaring silence in reality crept back to his psyche, delaying the words lodged at his tongue.   

Though *It* was nowhere to be found, the shadows in the library began to remind him of the shadows Georg once cast over that tower.  

**THUMP.**  

“…Apologies, it’s nothing. Farewell, I’ll…see you some other time.” Gerwyn felt shame in leaving the conversation on an awkward note.  

“Ah, well then, farewell, Lord Gerwyn.” Cen bowed to him as he walked off.  

Not wanting to bother them anymore that he felt he had, it was not long before he was past the wooden door out of the Archives and walking back up the pitch dark tunnel to the castle above.   

*Just do the usual and stay invisible.*

Further into the night, Victor Ashwright held a moment of respite in his room.   

Once again, The workaholic blood of his veins had exhausted him with meetings, planning, and grueling practice.   

*I have to make up for the mistakes back then. Curses, if only my stamina was boundless.*  

Unwilling to turn in yet, he mustered up what he had remaining into polishing his true sword, now an old relic of a decade long passed. Before he could gather total focus into it, a gentle yet rapid knocking came from the other side of his room’s door. From the sound alone, Victor knew the only one who could be visiting him at this hour.  

“Enter,” he said.  

Georg casually strode in, wearing his usual favorite outfit; a long, light silver trenchcoat with dark, cosmic fur and a black shirt of casual nobility. In one hand, he carried a large object completely wrapped in cloth and slung around his shoulder. “Hey there, little brother, how goes– ah, not that well. I see.”  

“State your business and be quick.”   

“What, do I need business to see you now?”   

Victor put his attention back on whetting his greatsword.   

“And here I figured you’d be over the moon.” Georg remarked while leaning back against a wall near the entrance and pulling out a leather flask filled with water. “Prime time to let loose now; Mercer gets exiled on the morrow.”  

“What is there to celebrate?”  

He took a small swig of the flask. “It’s what you’ve always wanted, no?”  

Victor frowned. “True, the man refuses to recant for such wanton violence; this exile was a long time coming after what he’s done. But it stands as trivial in the face of greater matters; I have scarce time to care for a rabid tiger being sent to the abyss above when these walls carry greater woes.”  

“Sure that’s the right mindset? I heard about the hissy fit you threw against Gerwyn and Pendra. Ohhhh, it’s been eons since Father and Mother were *that pissed* at you.”  

Victor clicked his tongue. “I recognize my methods back then as too harsh. But should I have said nothing? Is it wrong for me to advise our brethren on what they can do to improve their conduct?”  

“You’re ignoring the obvious in the room; Gerwyn of all people was the one to put him in his place.”  

Victor finished polishing the blade, becoming contemplative.  

“...He should not be fighting.”  

“Everyone’s got a reason to fight. Would you have preferred him to sit there and take it?”  

“Of course not. But it would have been preferable for him to not get involved in the first place. If the wind catches him fighting again, some will fancy him a warrior like the rest of us Ashwrights.”  

“...And?”  

“*And* he will be pushed into the role; do you not understand his condition? We cannot afford to send him to oblivion when Zantche still remains an open option.” Victor sheathed the polished greatsword and put it back up on the wall.  

Georg raised an eyebrow. “Zantche? What does that have to do with–” He stopped himself at a small realization. “Ah, never mind, don’t tell me. You plan to marry him off to Princess Qaine, first heir to that indecisive kingdom, so that they’ll be indebted to us as a vassal.”  

Victor took out a darker greatsword from the wall, and got to work with the whetstone again. “It is what fits him well. The prosperity of Zantche’s land will prove better for his health than the snow here. And we need a foothold in there before Lambda can do so.”  

Georg replied with haste. “It’ll provide its own challenges heavier than snow, though. Such as–”  

“I am aware of the caveats too, Georg.” Victor did not mean to interrupt, but it was too late to back out of his words now. “To send one of our own kin into territory that stands between us and Lambda is a mortal risk in its own right.”  

“And Lambda’s still got relations with Zantcheans here and there. Last I checked in King Hans’s court, most of them were confident in gathering enough allies for a united front. Knowing that, will you still force him there per the marriage?”  

“Our royal blood and name stands as high value. If all goes well and Gerwyn obeys, then his status alone should overwrite Lambda’s stake.”  

“Zantche is but one of a plethora. The rest of the North doesn’t like Lambda any more than us, so with enough time and change, we’ll likely win some favors back. Who knows, maybe Xaede or the nomads exiled from the Highlands will come back at our doorstep.”  

Victor set the blade down on the table and faced his brother.  

“...What are you playing at, Georg?”   

The eldest folded his arms and frowned. “What do you mean, ‘what am I playing at?’ Tis’ merely darts we throw at a board.”  

“Is this a ploy for me to underestimate our enemy?”   

“No? Why assume it is so?”  

“Those nomads are still Highlanders; hatred for our kind remains in their blood no matter how much we give to them. And after that tragedy at the Vanadis Border, Xaede's chances of coming back to us stand at–.”  

“A number still higher than zero; we've been over that bit, Victor. Also, it's in the blood of the Highlanders to hate everybody that isn't them, and those nomads they exiled haven't taken that well. Maybe if you went outside of here more, you'd see that.”   

Victor clenched a fist, but kept his voice steady. “Now that you stand as a double agent, your loyalties to the crown are at risk. How am I to believe this information is not curated to drop this demesne’s guard?”  

Georg had taken a swig of water out of his flask while Victor talked, then quickly answered him once he finished speaking. “Because I’m aiming for Lambda's downfall here too, little brother. Maybe I don’t share the same hatred of their people as you and Father do, but they still have no place being carved in history’s finest legends. If I have to sit in another few of those Lambdaein counsels with that blasted Arrogator *whilst* babysitting those *shitstains* spawned from his balls *whilst* listening to another one of those *god-cocksucking* fanatics that dare to call themselves advisors, I’m going to shove my head into the nearest blast forge, permanently.”  

“Are you–?”  

“Serious? Yes.” Georg successfully predicted Victor while stepping in closer. “No matter how much time will pass, nothing will sway my apostasy to the Seven. Least of all a kingdom that worships their every pore, and least of all one including that depraved mongrel they call *Neidhart*.”   

Such tricks of the tongue would have paralyzed any other, but Victor held firm in his questioning. To further combat any doubts in the mind, he picked back up the sword on the table and resumed the sharpening, whetstone fastened to his iron grip. “Lambda's fanaticism is sharpened more towards our downfall, no? I understand how you feel about the Seven, but it is difficult to believe that as enough of a reason you'd willingly stick to our side.” he said without pause to the craft.  

“Well, let’s not open the snake's crate of how they see the average Xaedean, since that proves my point *too* well.”  

Curious, Victor scowled. “...No, let's. If you speak of how the Xaedeans worship Zaiklonael of the Wind and Erde Tierkreis of the Earth through more natural means instead, then I fail to see how that's enough for prejudice.”  

Georg inhaled a long breath and chuckled. “Ohh, you have no idea how much more than enough it is, little brother. Mention Xaede elves around enough Lambdaeins, and one of them’s guaranteed to go red-eyed, frothing at the mouth ranting about their ‘unbalanced worship’ or ‘bizarre contraptions’ or ‘connections to The Devil with their knife ears’ or what have you. Honestly, I don't get it either, it's one of those things you'd have to see in person to believe.”  

“But King Hans bears no devout will for The Septiment.”   

“Most of his other counsel does, though. The majority wins out, and so does the worship of the rulers before him.”  

“...A fair point, brother, but the statement still stands. Xaede is not coming back to us. At best, they’ll stay out of our way, and at worst, they’ll interfere as some sort of ‘third party.’”  

“Not if you get *them* roped into an arranged marriage instead. Hmm, if I recall, Gerwyn was once close with a certain princess of the strongest clan there, the Lunendras. Speaking from personal experience, few things are better than a fair deal with their forest with a side of some good old elf se–”  

“Don’t. You. *Dare.*” Victor gritted his teeth, barely on the cusp of fury.  

Georg stepped back with his hands up. “Yeesh, take a hard sentence for once, little brother. What’s the problem?”  

Victor calmed himself with an iron clenching of a fist. “That ‘certain princess’ almost put his life in mortal peril. To bring them back together would be tantamount to ensuring his death. It’s too great of a risk.”  

“...But throwing him into Zantche, where there’s more Lambdaeins on the side than elves, isn’t?”   

Victor let the question hang in the air so he could regain his composure, then went back to sharpening his blades. “The first princess, Qaine, carries a proper reputation backed by kindness and intellect. All Gerwyn needs to do is play the part of a good spouse to her.”  

Georg shrugged in resignation. “There’s plenty to disagree with on your plan, but it’s a little too late in the night to be running circles. Executing this plan assumes Father and Mother will hear you out beforehand. Give it about…” Georg pondered for roughly 5 seconds. “Three weeks. Wait about three weeks, then tell them once they’re calm.”  

Victor had finished whetting the blade and sheathed it back in time to look directly at the eldest brother. “So the *rest of this month*? Georg, time is of the essence with–”  

“And thanks to your *fuckup* earlier, you’ll now have to use it for other things before they can trust your judgement again.” His voice sharpened.  

“...Apologies. You are right.” Victor lowered himself, forlorn.  

Georg closed his eyes again and brightened back up. “Ah, come now, don’t give that look, it’s nothing to wallow on. More importantly, here’s a belated birthday gift for you.” He began to unwrap the object wrapped in cloth.  

Victor, suspicious, furrowed his brow. “Ashwright celebrates no birthdays. That occasion is reserved for the Festival of the Northern Gradiance.”  

“Yes, well, I think relegating it all to the start of a new year where some lightshow in the skies comes out to play is getting a bit dated these days. Besides, it’s been years since I’ve given you something, so now’s a good time to remedy that.”  

Upon fully unwrapping the object, revealed to be a sword, Victor’s suspicion transformed into pure shock.   

The wide scabbard was of black leather adorned in gold and silver rings. Georg quickly unsheathed it, revealing its steel of sea-jade that gleamed in the dim room like a sun. The steel's various scratches and marks amplified the light like the night sky's brightest stars. The edge was at the perfect border between size and sharpness for a greatsword; Victor had seen its boundless potential for combat by the one who previously wielded it.  

“Wrøhsyr,” he whispered instinctively. “Why bring it *here*?”  

“Because it's yours now, obviously.” Georg grinned.  

For the first time in a while, Victor was absolutely flabbergasted. “*W-w-w-what\!?* Have you gone *senile*\!? You should know the *importance* of this sword better than anyone\!”  

“Aye, it’s because of that importance that it fits your hands better than any other weapon would.”  

“Have you forgotten how your lover, Ida, kept this close at her side\!? To use it so casually would be a disrespect to not only her wishes, but those of her elven brethren\! Must you force me to commit something so sinful\!?”  

“Well, I can’t confirm that, since she’s dead and all. But I know she would have preferred to see it used than collect dust. And besides, a future king and commander needs at least one blade befitting his station. Where’s the sin in that?”  

“Georg…whatever *high path* she chose to climb…it’s beyond–”  

Georg clicked his tongue. “Look, just take the *fucking* sword already. This thing’s killing my arm.”   

“...Fine.”  

Convinced at last, Victor took the sword by the handle. Raising the blade’s point upwards and facing the dim moonlight, he took a deep breath and declared a vow only he and Georg could hear.  

“I know not my worth in holding thy glaive, long ascended spirits of the past.
